## Cottage Construction Begins

The cottage construction started early with the creation of a paper model to size the footprint and location. With the plants all available for installation, attention moved to the construction of the cottage itself.

This cottage would be the centerpiece of the entire garden - a historic Victorian structure with authentic thatched roof, approximately 16cm height, 11cm width, and 19cm length (roof height 9.5m at scale).

## Building the Structure

### Wall Construction

The walls were all cut out of balsa wood and covered with a layer of filler to prepare the wall texture. The walls were all largely completed and textured before assembly - a lesson learned from previous projects to ensure even finishing.

### Chimney Construction

The chimney construction was incorporated into the wall structure. The wall texture was modified to have a brick effect, and the chimney itself was made from balsa wood covered in filler to allow for the brick effect of the chimney.

The chimney was then glued to the inside of the wall and incorporated into the roof structure. This integrated approach ensured the chimney would be structurally sound and properly positioned relative to the roof peak.

## The Thatched Roof

Next was the construction of the thatched roof - the feature that distinguished this cottage and required learning entirely new techniques.

### Research and YouTube Learning

Several YouTube videos were very informative. I borrowed some ideas from them, but ultimately chose to use real grass:

- [Thatched Cottage Tutorial 1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rPA61burEnY)
- [Thatched Cottage Tutorial 2](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QK4g8IgfkBQ)
- [Thatched Cottage Tutorial 3](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bXXjanD1_JE)

These tutorials showed various approaches, but I wanted to use natural materials to achieve the most realistic effect at this scale.

### Grass Collection and Preparation

Grass from the garden was collected, sorted, and cut into small bundles. One of the key challenges encountered was sorting and bundling the grass. After struggling for some time, I engineered a V-shaped caddy to feed the grass on one side, sort out the bits not required, and then pick a bundle with all the grass pointing in the same direction.

### Applying the Thatch

Grass bundles were glued starting from the bottom, working upward in overlapping layers to create the characteristic thatched appearance. This method mimics real thatching technique where each layer overlaps the one below.

<div style="text-align: center; margin: 20px 0;">
  <img src="{{ '/assets/images/projects/english-country-garden/cottage/ecg-cot-011.jpg' | relative_url }}" alt="Thatching in progress" style="max-width: 100%;">
  <div class="caption">Grass bundles being applied from bottom upward</div>
</div>

The process was painstaking - each bundle had to be:
1. Selected from the sorted grass stock
2. Trimmed to appropriate length
3. Positioned to overlap correctly
4. Glued securely
5. Trimmed again for even appearance

Getting the grass smooth and cut exactly as a life-size roof would be was a significant challenge. The custom V-shaped sorting caddy helped enormously with this process.

## Cottage Construction Challenges

**Thatched Roof Technique:** The most significant challenge was achieving a realistic thatch appearance at miniature scale. Real grass provided authenticity but required the development of custom tools (V-shaped sorting caddy) to manage.

**Wall Texture Balance:** Getting the right amount of filler to create texture without obscuring balsa wood grain or adding too much weight required experimentation.

**Window Scale:** Creating convincing multi-pane sash windows at 1:50 scale with paper and plastic was delicate work requiring steady hands and precision cutting.

**Chimney Integration:** Ensuring the chimney was structurally sound while appearing to emerge naturally from the roof peak required planning during wall construction phase.

**Material Consistency:** Balancing weight, strength, and appearance across all cottage components while maintaining structural integrity.

## Weathering Approach

As noted in the project approach, weathering was woven into construction and coloring rather than being a separate phase. During painting and assembly:
- Wall colors included variations and subtle discoloration
- Window frames showed slight wear
- Thatch received varied tones suggesting age and weather exposure
- Brick chimney included mortar variations and slight staining

This integrated approach to weathering created a naturally aged appearance rather than adding effects after completion.

The combination of scratch-built construction, authentic thatching with real grass, and period-appropriate details created a structure worthy of the over 100 handcrafted plants that would soon fill the garden around it.
